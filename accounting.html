Inflow and Outflow

  • Inflow - Income (money coming in that isn't repayable), Debt/Liabilities (money coming in that is repayable), Selling Asset
  • Outflow - Expense (Something you're spending money on that you are getting the rewards of instantly, you don't get the money back), Assets (Something you're spending money on that you will get rewards of in long term and you get the money back - recoverable, whatever a firm holds and uses for a long period), Clearing Liability
  • Classification of transactions happens in these 6 areas ^
  • Income - Not repayable inflow
  • Liability - Repayable inflow
  • Expense - Not recoverable outflow
  • Assets - Recoverable in the future

Accounting principles

Entity principle

business owner and business are separate entities

  • the profits minus dividends are shown as a liability to the owner

going concern principle

  • each company is an ongoing operation - accounting is to be done with the idea that the company will never close down

Money measurement principle:

  • Every transaction needs monetary term - no bartering

Cost

  • In the books of accounting, everything is accounted for at historical costs only
  • No notional or market values

Matching principle

  • whatever you're earning has to be matched - P&L is accurately depicted - transactions must be accounted for when its coming in

Period principle

  • Accounting for things in the right period

Accrual principle

  • you will account for a transaction even if actual cash isn't coming in
  • If it's coming in then account for it for sure
  • receivables are accounted for when they are accounted for not when they aren't
    Treat every transaction in isolation
    receivable account exists brother

Materiality

  • anything that is significant enough for decision making should be disclosed separately
  • If any transaction is above a certain threshold, it has to be shown separately in the reports - the thresholds are set by the companies act
  • Some classifications have to be disclosed separately even if they aren't above the thresholds set by the companies act
  • any major event has to be disclosed

Consistency

  • there are accounting standards - international, indian, etc.
  • bunch of rules that tell you how to account for different stuff
  • Although you are free to choose a standard, stick with it

Conservatism

  • You cannot record for a notional or forseeable profit but you must record for forseeable losses
  • Basically if you're happy, stop being happy because accounting doesn't want that
  • inventory is products unsold by the end of the year - matching principle says what didn't get sold cannot be taken as an expense. inventory is an asset
  • inventory is to be valued at market value or net realizable value, whichever is lower - exception to cost principle

Owner's funds

Debtor/Creditor

  • Debtor is someone who owns money to you - usually someone you've sold goods on credit
  • Other terms for debtor are - receivables, trade receivables, account receivables
  • Creditor is the person from whom the company has a debt from. Generally the vendor from whom they've bought stuff on credit
  • Payables, trade payables, accounts payables
  • Debtor is asset and creditor is liability

Insolvency

  • Solvent - A person who is in a position to pay his debts as they become due
  • Insolvent - A person who is not in a position to pay his debts as they become due

  • If, later on the customer gives the money, you account it as an income
  • Following the principle of conservatism, companies are required to keep a provision for "doubtful" debts

Tea Stall

Say soma starts a tea stall, he brings in 1000 rupees and borrows 1000 rupees. It is borrowed on the condition that at the end of every month he will have to pay 300 rupees. That's all he can borrow at the present
Soma starts with 2000 rupees. Total capital employed is 1000 rupees (owner's funds/ shareholders funds) and 1000 rupees of borrowed money (debt/borrowings)
Hot plate, vessels, crockery, etc is bought by soma for 1000 rupees. These are fixed assets.
Then He buys his raw material like tea, milk, etc. These are his stock/inventory of working capital for 500 rupees. 500 rupees is left
On day 1 he sells 100 cups of tea at 5 rupees each. The tradition in the area is that you're paid the next day. Soma hasn't received a single rupee yet but he knows he will receive tomorrow(accounts receivable)
He is left with 200 rupees worth of stock which is usable on the next day
He has hired a helper wjo will cost him 50 rupees a day. The helpers salary will be paid on a weekly basis. Besides,he has to pay 10 rupees as interest on the loan
Soma has not recieved a single penny at the end of day 1, but we know that he generated sales of 500 rupees and material cost was only 300 rupees. In other words,his gross profit was 200 rupees. Sales-direct revenue expenditure = gross profit
We are taking into account sales and expenses only without any cash coming in, this is called the accrual principle
Consider material cost on day 1. Logically,we take material cost at 300 rupees
If soma has to close his business the next day, his fixed assets will barely sell for 200 rupees, so do we take this as a loss of 800 rupees? no cus going converna and all so we gotta keep positive
If at the end of the day someone promises you that he will buy 200 cups at 6 rupees, you cant mark that as a profit cus conservatism

Accounting cycle

  • The process of accounting:
  • Identifying the event
  • converting the event into monetary terms
  • recording the journal entry
  • posting to ledger account - its a place where all the accounts are kept, so that you know whether you're insolvent or not
  • Preparing the trial balance - this is a quick check of whether your accounts are correct and you can check this using the credit and debit totalling 0. If they don't, you need to fix a few things and adjust some transactions
  • Preparing the final acounts: Profit and loss account and balance sheet
